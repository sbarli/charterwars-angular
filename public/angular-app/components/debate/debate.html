So how do we create a public education system that provides equal opportunity? How do we ensure that quality education is a right for all, not a privilege for few? Education reformers believe that the current system has not, cannot, and never will achieve that goal. Instead, education reformers look to the private sector for inspiration.
In a charter-wide model, schools act as independent operators in a free market where families are the consumers, and district bureaucracy gives way to the forces of choice, competition and innovation, building a more efficient and equitable system in its place.

“The real debate isn’t between charter or district, but rather a debate over whom we chose to listen to, and what we chose to believe.”
– Jon Cetel, Pro-Charter Activist

But what if that model rests on a faulty assumption? Pro-district advocates argue that economic market forces alone have not, cannot, and never will balance out existing inequity in our public education.
In more concrete terms, what happens to the families and communities vulnerable to substandard public education? What happens when a school relocates out of a neighborhood? What happens when schools pursue both quality education and financial profits?
These ‘what-ifs’ aren’t just fodder for intellectual debate, they underpin the policies governing the education of 50 million children in America.
